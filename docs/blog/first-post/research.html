Intellectuals, Ideas and Support for the French Revolution

Extended Abstract

The influence of ideas on social, political, and economic change has been a central theme in the social sciences. In his foundational work, Tocqueville (1856) emphasized how the spread of Enlightenment ideas, especially those promoting individual rights and challenging absolute monarchy, helped foster the political climate for the French Revolution. Tocqueville highlighted the role of intellectuals in disseminating these revolutionary ideas, disintegrating old beliefs, and providing legitimacy for radical change. Despite the longstanding recognition of the importance of ideas, the extent of their influence on the Revolution remains contested among historians (Burrows 2014). Building on this rich historiographical tradition, this study seeks to deepen our understanding of how intellectual movements influenced political and social change, using the key case study of revolutionary France.

The French Revolution stands as a pivotal moment in history, fundamentally reshaping the political and social order of its time. It championed the universal principles of human rights, dismantled deeply entrenched estates systems, asserting the equality of all individuals before the law. It also laid the groundwork for the separation of church and state, fostering ideals of secular governance. These transformative changes set a global precedent, inspiring movements across the world. Given the Revolution’s distinctive features—and the relative scarcity of quantitative analyses of the phenomenon—it provides a compelling case study for examining how ideas can drive institutional change. Building on this context, the goal of this paper is to empirically test the role of Enlightenment ideas in shaping support for the French Revolution.

In this paper, I test the influence of ideas through two main channels. First, I examine the role of intellectuals as key figures in spreading Enlightenment ideals. Second, I explore how books—particularly works of philosophy and novels—helped inspire shifts in values and attitudes.

To do this, I rely on a broad set of data sources. I measure the circulation of ideas using biographical information on intellectuals (Laouenan et al. 2022) and book sales data from the Société Typographique de Neuchâtel (Burrows 2020), a major supplier to the French market. To capture support for the Revolution, I digitised data on the foundation and location of political societies, the proto-political parties that emerged during the revolutionary period. I also digitised the Cahiers de Doléances, the lists of grievances compiled by all electoral territories (bailliages) before the 1789 elections, categorised by Hyslop (1934), to identify demands for political and social change across local assemblies. Finally, I incorporate data on collective violence during 1789 (Chambru and Maneuvrier-Hervieu 2024).

Econometrically, I test the role of ideas by exploiting geographical variation in the presence of intellectuals, book circulation, and revolutionary support. To address endogeneity concerns, I implement several strategies. First, I use distance from Neuchâtel—the STN’s printing center—as an instrument for exposure to books, relying on a gravity model of trade where distance reduces trade intensity, and leveraging Neuchâtel’s peripheral position in French history to support the exclusion restriction. Second, I conduct falsification tests using the Cahiers de Doléances to verify that intellectual or book presence does not also predict demands for economic liberalization, addressing concerns related to modernization theory. Third, I reclassify book categories, showing that only exposure to philosophy and novels predicts revolutionary support, and not book readings in general.

This project contributes to the literature in several ways. First, it provides new quantitative insights into the French Revolution, to this day still limited. It also contributes by digitizing key historical sources. Additionally, the study highlights the role of intellectuals as political actors (Kurzman 2008) and assesses the empirical basis of claims regarding their influence on institutional change. Finally, by empirically evaluating these arguments, the project offers insights into the process of modern institutional formation, emphasizing the importance of intellectuals for economic growth (Mokyr 2011; Squicciarini and Voigtländer 2015).
